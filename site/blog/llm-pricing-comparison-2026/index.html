Provider-by-Provider Breakdown

OpenAI

OpenAI's April 2026 lineup centers on the GPT-4.1 family. The full OpenAI pricing breakdown covers every model, but the key insight is the new tiered approach: Nano for high-volume, low-complexity tasks, Mini for balanced workloads, and the full 4.1 for tasks requiring maximum capability. The o-series models (o3, o4-mini) add reasoning capabilities at slightly different price points.

OpenAI offers a generous free tier through their API with rate limits. Batch processing gets a 50% discount on all models.

Anthropic

Anthropic's Claude lineup spans from Haiku 4.5 (their cost-optimized model at $0.80/$4.00 per 1M tokens) to Opus 4.6 (their most capable model at $15/$75). See our detailed Anthropic pricing page for tier breakdowns and prompt caching discounts.

Claude's prompt caching feature reduces costs by up to 90% for repeated context. If you are building applications with large system prompts, this changes the economics significantly.

Google

Gemini 2.5 Pro offers strong performance at $1.25/$10.00 per 1M tokens with a massive 1M token context window. Gemini 2.5 Flash at $0.15/$0.60 competes directly with GPT-4.1 Nano on price while offering a larger context window. Google also provides generous free tiers through AI Studio.

Open Source (Llama, Mistral)

Meta's Llama 4 models are free to use if you self-host, but most teams access them through inference providers like Together AI, Fireworks, or AWS Bedrock. Provider pricing varies but typically runs $0.10-0.30 per 1M input tokens for Scout-class models.

Mistral offers both API access and self-hosting options. Their Small model at $0.10/$0.30 per 1M tokens is one of the cheapest commercial API options available.

Hidden Costs Nobody Mentions

Output tokens cost more than input tokens

Every provider charges 2-5x more for output tokens than input tokens. This means generation-heavy workloads (writing, coding, summarization) cost significantly more than classification or extraction tasks where outputs are short.

Context window usage affects cost

Sending a 100K token document with every API call gets expensive fast, even with cheap models. Prompt caching (available from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google) mitigates this, but only if your prompts have a consistent prefix.

Rate limits force tier upgrades

Free and low tiers have aggressive rate limits. Production workloads almost always require paid tiers with higher throughput. Check each provider's rate limit documentation before committing to a model based on per-token pricing alone.

Which Model Should You Use?

The right model depends on your use case, not just the price tag.

  • High-volume classification or extraction: GPT-4.1 Nano or Gemini 2.5 Flash. Both deliver strong results at under $0.50 per 1M tokens.
  • General-purpose chatbots and assistants: GPT-4.1 Mini or Claude Haiku 4.5. Good quality at moderate cost.
  • Complex reasoning and coding: Claude Sonnet 4.6 or GPT-4.1. The quality difference over cheaper models is measurable on hard tasks.
  • Maximum capability (research, complex analysis): Claude Opus 4.6 or o3. Expensive but meaningfully better on the hardest tasks.
  • Budget-constrained with quality needs: Llama 4 via inference providers or Mistral Small. Surprisingly capable for the price.

Pricing Trends: What Changed in 2026

Three trends are reshaping LLM pricing in 2026:

  1. Context windows are expanding faster than prices are dropping. GPT-4.1 and Gemini 2.5 both offer 1M token windows. Llama 4 Scout supports 10M tokens. Larger contexts mean more tokens per request, which can offset per-token price drops.
  2. Reasoning models are a new pricing tier. OpenAI's o-series and other reasoning-focused models sit between standard and premium pricing, creating a middle tier that didn't exist in 2025.
  3. Caching and batching discounts are becoming standard. Prompt caching, batch processing, and volume discounts make list prices less meaningful. The effective cost for optimized workloads is often 30-60% below list price.
